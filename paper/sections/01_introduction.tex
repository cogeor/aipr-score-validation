\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Peer review is the gate through which scientific work passes, and it is under
strain: submission volumes at major venues have grown faster than the reviewer
pool, and the process is demonstrably noisy. The NeurIPS consistency experiments
found that a large fraction of accept/reject decisions would flip under a re-run
of the same process \citep{cortes2021inconsistency,beygelzimer2023arbitrary},
with the asymmetry that review is comparatively reliable at \emph{rejecting weak
work} but poor at \emph{ranking strong work}. This is the backdrop against which
automated, LLM-based assistance is now being proposed and used.

Most studies of automated review evaluate the \emph{text} a model produces: how
well its comments overlap a human reviewer's, how specific or factual they are
\citep{liang2024llm,darcy2024marg,yuan2022automate,du2024llmsassist}. A
complementary and, for deployment, more consequential question is whether the
\emph{score} such a system assigns is a valid signal of the quality humans
ultimately perceive. A score is what triages a queue, flags a submission for a
second look, or summarizes a first pass; used that way, it must be validated as a
measurement against an external outcome, with the discipline the noisy ground
truth demands.

We study \textsc{AIPR}, a system that reads a submitted PDF and produces five
0--100 quality dimensions (novelty, rigor, applicability, clarity, and citation)
and a weighted overall score (\S\ref{sec:methods}). In deployment AIPR is
interactive: a reviewer questions its findings, requests evidence, and refines the
review in dialogue. This paper isolates the \emph{baseline} score the system emits
on first read, before any interaction, which is the hardest case for an automated
signal and the one a triage use relies on. Our ground truth is the public record
of a major machine learning venue on OpenReview: the decision tier of each
submission (reject, poster, oral) and the mean numeric rating its
reviewers assigned. Because this venue publishes \emph{rejected} submissions, the
class our central claim concerns is fully observable.

This paper asks two questions. The first is whether the score is \emph{valid}:
does it agree with the human outcome? Our claim here is narrow. The AIPR score (i)
flags weak submissions, in that papers it scores low are rejected at far above the
base rate, and (ii) shows strong, not merely significant, agreement with human
review outcomes across both the discrete decision tiers and the continuous reviewer
rating. We do \emph{not} claim it predicts the acceptance decision, ranks strong papers, or
substitutes for a reviewer. The bound follows the empirical ``reliable at the
bottom, noisy at the top'' structure of human review itself, and it is the use a
deployment can support.

The second question is what the \emph{system} adds over the model it runs on, and
its answer is the paper's central result. A frontier model is available to anyone,
so we test AIPR against a generic one-paragraph prompt on the identical model and
PDF. The bare prompt, with no rubric and no audit, already separates rejected from
accepted submissions on its own, and the full pipeline discriminates no better: the
two are at parity. The lesson is about the model, not the pipeline---\emph{with or
without our prompt, a frontier model already produces a first-pass reviewing signal
that substantially tracks the human outcome in this setting, so raw model
intelligence is not the observed bottleneck}, and the reviewer keeps the decision. What the engineered pipeline---a scored rubric, a
grounded citation audit, and a two-pass read---adds is therefore not validity but
\emph{deployability}: a score that is reliable across repeated runs and arrives with
a grounded, anchored review, produced in a single pass. This pre-registered
comparison, not the headline correlation, is what the study turns on.

Three practices set the study's standard of evidence. First, we pre-registered the
hypotheses, the primary metric, and the low-score threshold before any score was
joined to any outcome; the pre-registration is included verbatim
(Appendix~\ref{app:prereg}). Second, every number in this paper is generated from
the data by a single script, with no figure or statistic transcribed by hand, so
the analysis is reproducible end to end from the released table. Third, we report
the score's failure modes (\S\ref{sec:failure}): the rejected papers it rated
highly and the unconventional accepted papers it underrated, with the human
reviews that explain each. Together---a design frozen and pre-registered before
unblinding, an analysis any reader can re-run from the released table, and an
openly catalogued set of failure modes---these are the discipline that lets a
single author's validation of a proprietary instrument be checked rather than
trusted.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
\begin{enumerate}\itemsep2pt
  \item A pre-registered, reproducible validation of a structured LLM quality
  \emph{score} against two complementary human ground truths (ordinal decision
  tiers and continuous reviewer ratings) at the scale of \NminiPrimary{}
  submissions.
  \item Evidence for a bounded, deployable claim: weak submissions are
  consistently flagged (lowest score quintile rejected at \bottomRejectRate\%, a
  \bottomLift$\times$ lift), distinguished from the stronger claims it is often
  conflated with.
  \item A pre-registered comparison showing that the validity comes from the model,
  not the pipeline: a bare one-paragraph prompt on the same model and PDF already
  separates rejected from accepted submissions (AUROC \aurocNaiveFull) and the full
  pipeline does no better (paired $\Delta$AUROC \aurocDiffFullNaive, \aurocDiffPrel),
  so a frontier model already carries much of the outcome-aligned judgment signal
  with or without our prompt---raw intelligence is not the observed bottleneck. What the pipeline adds is
  consistency (within-paper SD \fullRunSD{} vs.\ \naiveRunSD{} points) and a
  grounded, anchored review in the same pass.
  \item A cost design in which the full pipeline on a cheap model establishes the
  relationship at scale and a frontier-model cohort confirms it, with the
  mini-to-frontier agreement measured ($\rho =$~\bridgeRhoFull), not assumed.
  \item An analysis of the score's two error classes and their asymmetric
  consequences for a triage use---a false negative at the top of the range is the
  costly case and is quantified directly, while a false positive costs only a
  second human look---with matched, verbatim human-review case studies for each
  pattern (\S\ref{sec:failure}).
\end{enumerate}
