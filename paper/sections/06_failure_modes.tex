\section{Failure Modes}
\label{sec:failure}

A score that is useful in deployment must be characterized where it errs as well
as where it succeeds. We examine both directions of error on the frontier cohort,
and because the venue is open we read the actual human reviews to explain each.
The case studies are selected to span the recurring patterns.

\subsection{What the score catches (and why)}
For rejected submissions the score rated low, the in-depth review's stated
weaknesses align closely with the reasons human reviewers gave.
Table~\ref{tab:casestudy} (Appendix~\ref{app:cases}) places the AIPR weaknesses
and omitted-citation findings beside excerpts from the human reviews for a set of
matched examples. The recurring, correctly-flagged patterns are:
\begin{itemize}\itemsep1pt
  \item \textbf{Low novelty / derivative work}: the score's novelty dimension is
  low and the audit pass surfaces the closely related prior method the
  submission did not cite, the same omission reviewers raised.
  \item \textbf{Thin or missing baselines}: the rigor dimension is low with a
  justification naming the absent comparison.
  \item \textbf{Overclaiming}: a gap between stated contribution and supporting
  evidence, flagged in the rigor and applicability justifications.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Where the score is wrong}
We report both error classes explicitly.
\paragraph{False negatives: rejected work the score rated highly.}

The dominant expected cause is a class of defect the system structurally cannot
see: the score evaluates the manuscript as written and cannot execute an
experiment, reproduce a result, or re-derive a proof. Where rejection hinges on
such a defect, a high score is expected and is not evidence against the bounded
claim of \S\ref{sec:results}.

\paragraph{False positives: strong work the score underrated.}
% PLACEHOLDER (real data): characterize the oral/spotlight papers in the lower

% papers penalized on the applicability dimension, or sub-fields under-represented

These cases concentrate where human reviewers reward what an automated reader
discounts: unconventional framing, or theoretical contributions whose real-world
applicability is not the point. They illustrate the ``poor at ranking the top''
asymmetry and bound any use of the score for positive selection.

\subsection{Reading of the failure modes}
The two error classes differ in their consequences for deployment. A false
negative at the \emph{top} of the score range (a weak paper scored high) is the
dangerous case for a triage tool, and we quantify its rate directly in the
reliability curve (Fig.~\ref{fig:validation}b). A false positive (a strong paper
scored low) costs a second human look, which is the intended action anyway. This
asymmetry is what makes the narrow claim, to flag weak work for human attention,
defensible as a human-reviewed triage signal under the studied conditions even
where the score ranks strong work poorly.
