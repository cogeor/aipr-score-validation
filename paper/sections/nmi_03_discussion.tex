\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:nmi-discussion}
% Alias label so the reused appendix (SI) cross-references resolve in this variant.
\label{sec:discussion}

The load-bearing result is that a bare one-paragraph prompt on the frontier model
already separates rejected from accepted submissions on its own (AUROC
\aurocNaiveFull), and the engineered pipeline's higher AUROC is not statistically
resolved against it. Read that way, the result points to a different bottleneck:
\emph{intelligence is not the observed bottleneck}. The model can already produce an
outcome-aligned first-pass score; what a deployment is short of is not raw judgment
but stability, grounding, and accountable presentation. The open problem is turning
that capability into an instrument a reviewer can rely on---one that returns the same
verdict on the same paper across runs, grounds its claims in retrieved evidence
rather than generating them, and surfaces an anchored, rubric-structured review
instead of a bare number. That is an engineering problem, and it is the one AIPR
addresses; the reliability gap and the grounded review, not a higher score, are its
demonstrated contribution. The implication for the field is that effort is better
spent on the processing layer---reliability, grounding, interaction---than on waiting
for a more intelligent base model the task does not appear to need.

This reframes the policy question. Recent randomized evidence shows AI feedback can
improve review \emph{text} \citep{thakkar2026feedback}; our complementary result is
that a first-pass \emph{score} can be criterion-valid against human outcomes. Neither
licenses uncontrolled use: the prevalence of undisclosed AI-assisted review
\citep{russolatona2024lottery} and adversarial hidden prompts in manuscripts
\citep{lin2025hidden} are exactly why assistance must be a controlled, disclosed,
auditable system rather than reviewers pasting confidential manuscripts into general
tools. The evidence argues against categorical rejection of AI assistance and for
governing and evaluating it, while keeping consequential decisions with humans. The
validated action here is prioritizing manuscripts for human attention, not automated
rejection.

\label{sec:limits}%
Several limits bound the claim, and we state them plainly rather than repeatedly.
The evidence is within a single venue family in one field; we do not claim cross-field
generalization, and a two-year within-venue temporal contrast is a planned follow-up
(\S\ref{sec:nmi-methods}). The scorer is proprietary: the analysis, labels, and
de-identified scores are released and fully reproducible, but the manuscript-to-score
function is audited through its outputs rather than re-implemented, with prompts and
configuration available confidentially to editors. Contamination from outcome
memorization is controlled by construction---the grading model's (\texttt{gpt-5.4})
August~2025 knowledge cutoff precedes the January~2026 release of the cohort's
decisions and reviews---and bounded further by an arXiv-before-cutoff sensitivity
split; topic priors and preprint familiarity remain residual risks. The citation
subscore is isolated, not validated here, and does not enter the headline. Finally,
the human ground truth is itself noisy and increasingly AI-assisted, which is why we
validate against the mean reviewer rating and lead with the robust low-end claim
rather than ranking the top.
