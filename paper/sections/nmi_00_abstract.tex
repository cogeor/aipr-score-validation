\begin{abstract}
\noindent
Large language models are increasingly proposed to assist peer review, but most
evaluations judge the \emph{text} of machine-generated reviews rather than the
validity of the numeric \emph{score} a system assigns. We validate \textsc{AIPR},
which reads a manuscript and emits a weighted 0--100 score, against the public
outcomes at a major machine-learning venue. Across \NminiPrimary{} ICLR submissions,
with hypotheses pre-registered before any score met any outcome, the score separates
rejected from accepted submissions (AUROC~\aurocMini), rises across decision tiers,
and tracks reviewer ratings; it is strongest as a low-score triage flag (bottom
quintile rejected at \bottomRejectRateFrontier\% by the production model). A generic one-paragraph prompt
on the same model already carries much of this signal (AUROC~\aurocNaive):
intelligence is not the observed bottleneck. What the engineering adds is not
validity but deployability---a score reliable across runs that arrives with a
grounded, auditable review. The validated action is prioritizing manuscripts for
human attention, not automated decisions.
\end{abstract}
