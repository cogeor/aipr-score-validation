\begin{abstract}
\noindent
Large language models are proposed to assist peer review, yet most evaluations judge the
prose of machine-generated review \emph{text}, not the validity of the numeric \emph{score}
it assigns. We validate \textsc{AIPR}, which emits a weighted 0--100 score across five
dimensions, against the public decisions of a major machine-learning venue, by prompting
alone, without fine-tuning. With hypotheses pre-registered before any score met any outcome,
the production score separates rejected from accepted submissions (AUROC~\aurocFull),
replicated on a \NminiPrimary-paper cohort (\aurocMini); it rises across decision tiers,
tracks reviewer ratings, and flags weak work, its lowest-scoring fifth rejected far above the
base rate. Validity comes mostly from the model: a one-paragraph prompt discriminates nearly
as well (gap favouring the pipeline but missing our pre-declared criterion, \aurocDiffPrel).
What the engineering adds is \emph{reliability} and a grounded review: AIPR's score barely
moves across runs where the bare prompt swings, the human keeping the decision.
\end{abstract}
